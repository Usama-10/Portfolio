What is Pond?

A pond is a small area of still freshwater. Ponds usually form when water begins to fill in a depression in the ground.

Ponds differs from lakes or streams because they do not have moving water and they also consists of a small area around 1.8m unlike lakes or streams.

Some ponds are formed naturally, filled by an underwater spring or by rainwater, sometimes also known as “dewponds”; on the other hand, other ponds are artificially made.

Ponds are usually quite shallow water bodies with varying abundances of aquatic plants such as water lily, duckweeds, and animals.

Ponds can be divided into four main zones: vegetation zone, open water, bottom mud and surface film.

Ponds are not permanent, over time, sometimes hundreds of years, ponds can change into marshes and swamps and then back to land.

The temperature of water present in the pond is usually about the same from the top to the bottom and it varies with air temperature. Ponds can also freeze solid from top to bottom in extremely cold places.

Ponds are found in gardens, towns and villages, countryside on farmlands, floodplains, in woods, on grasslands and on moors.

 

What is Lake?

A lake is a very slow flowing consistent body of open water, localized in a basin, surrounded by land. They can be contrasted with rivers or streams.

Many lakes are artificial and are constructed for agricultural or industrial use, for hydroelectric power generation or water supply, or for recreational purposes.

Natural lakes can be found be in mountainous areas, rift zones, and some areas with ongoing glaciation.

Lakes also vary greatly in depth. The world’s deepest lake is Lake Baikal, in Russia. It’s bottom in nearly 2 kilometers (more than 1 mile) below the surface in places.

The water that lakes contain usually comes from rain, snow, melting ice, streams and groundwater seepage and most lakes contain fresh water.

Lakes can also form due to Volcanoes. After a volcano becomes inactive, it’s crater may fill with rain or melted snow. They may also be formed by landslides that send soil, rock or mud sliding down mountains or hills. The debris then piles up in natural dams that blocks the flow of a stream, forming a Lake.

All lakes are not permanent like humans, they age with time, over the course of hundreds of years until disappearing from the land.

Difference between Ponds and Lakes

1)    Ponds have closer contact with adjacent territorial environment and thus higher terrestrial-aquatic interchange of organisms and matter unlike lakes which have relatively low number of organisms and matter present in them.

2)     Ponds are potentially more isolated from other wetlands and have a more insular nature compared with the large catchments and riverine inflows of lakes.

3)    Lakes can be as deep as 4000+ deep, while most ponds are shallow.

4)    Flora and Fauna are typically more diverse in ponds than in lakes.

5)    Lakes are usually freshwater but, in some cases, can contain some amounts of salt water while ponds only contain freshwater.

 

Comparison Between Ponds and Lakes

 

Parameters of Comparison

Ponds

Lakes

Depth

A pond is only 4-20 feet deep.

A lake is 20-4000 feet deep.

Outlet

Ponds are usually closed bodies of water.

Lakes are open bodies of water.

Size

Ponds are smaller than 200 acres of land.

Lakes can go beyond than 200+ acres of land.

Temperature

Ponds have consistent temperature throughout.

Lakes are typically warmer at the surface, and colder in deeper waters.

Sunlight

Ponds have full sunlight penetration throughout.

Lakes consists of aphotic zones (regions of darkness)
